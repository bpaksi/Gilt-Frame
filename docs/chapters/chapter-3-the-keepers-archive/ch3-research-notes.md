## The Central Artifact: Cassatt's Lost Mural

### "Modern Woman" (1893) — The Missing Masterpiece

- **Commission**: In 1891, Palmer personally commissioned Cassatt to paint a massive 12' × 58' mural for the Women's Building at the 1893 World's Columbian Exposition in Chicago.
- **Subject**: Allegorical triptych. Central panel: *Young Women Plucking the Fruits of Knowledge or Science*. Left: *Young Girls Pursuing Fame*. Right: *Arts, Music, Dancing*.
- **Cassatt's intent**: "An American friend asked me in rather a huffy tone, 'Then this is woman apart from her relations to man?' I told him it was."
- **Reception**: Critics noted "brilliancy of color" — yellow-greens, blues, deep plum — but dismissed the Impressionist palette as "unnatural." Even Degas criticized her.
- **Disappearance**: After the Fair, the mural came into Palmer's possession. Last documented ~1911. After Palmer's death in 1918, it vanished completely. No one has seen it in over 100 years.
- **Search**: Dr. Sally Webster wrote *Eve's Daughter/Modern Woman: A Mural by Mary Cassatt* attempting to track it down. Best theory: donated to a college, either hanging unidentified or rolled up in storage.
- **No record of destruction exists.**

### Order Narrative Potential

Palmer didn't lose the mural. She hid it. She knew what was coming (cancer, 1918 — the KILL phase). She secured the most important artifact Cassatt ever created — a painting literally about women passing knowledge to the next generation. That's what the Order does.

The mural's subject — women picking fruit from a "tree of knowledge" and passing it forward — IS the Order's mission encoded in a 58-foot canvas.

### The Reader — Visual Details (Verified from Multiple Sources)

- **Subject**: Young woman seated, absorbed in reading. Profile view, turned toward viewer.
- **What she holds**: A book with a bent cover ("suggesting it is a favorite")
- **Clothing**: Voluminous white garment (casual elegance). Red-accented hat (vibrant contrast).
- **Palette**: Muted — whites, soft greens, with the red hat as the key contrast.

## The Degas Sub-Text at Crystal Bridges

### Degas Was Briefly in the Vault

Crystal Bridges hosted **The William S. Paley Collection: A Taste for Modernism** — a traveling exhibition from MoMA that included works by Degas, Gauguin, Matisse, Cézanne, and Picasso. Crystal Bridges was the **only venue in the central United States** to host the exhibition, and the **last stop** before the works returned to MoMA.

Degas's work was temporarily in the same building as Cassatt's. It didn't stay.

**Order sub-text**: The painter's partner's work passed through this vault but wasn't kept. It moved on. Where did it end up? This gives Christine a reason to notice Degas at Crystal Bridges — not as a permanent resident, but as a visitor who left a trace. Past exhibition references, a catalog in the gift shop, or educational materials about Cassatt's biography will mention Degas.

### How Christine Discovers Degas (Visit 2)

Christine doesn't find a Degas painting. She finds Degas's NAME in the story of Cassatt. Crystal Bridges' gallery labels, audio tour (SoundCloud), and educational materials all mention:

- Cassatt was the only American invited to exhibit with the French Impressionists

- This happened in **1877** — the same year she painted The Reader

The Order asks Christine to answer two multiple-choice questions from the gallery labels:

**Q1**: "Who invited the painter to exhibit with the Impressionists?" → Edgar Degas

Christine's answer to Q1 — naming Degas — is the intelligence the Order needs. The Order takes that name, does its own research, discovers Degas worked in bronze, and traces his sculptures to Meijer Gardens in Grand Rapids.

## Exit Thread: Crystal Bridges → Meijer Gardens (Grand Rapids)

### The Cassatt → Degas → Lena Meijer Thread

**The artistic partnership**: Mary Cassatt and Edgar Degas were lifelong artistic partners. Cassatt was the only American to exhibit with the French Impressionists — at Degas's personal invitation. They influenced each other's work for decades.

**Degas at Meijer Gardens**: Frederik Meijer Gardens & Sculpture Park in Grand Rapids has Degas bronzes in the permanent collection, displayed among the arid and Victorian gardens. The collection includes works from Degas, Rodin, Henry Moore, Louise Bourgeois, and others.

**Lena Meijer** (1919–2022): The woman behind the gardens.
- Born on a farm near Lakeview, Michigan
- Hired as a cashier at the original Meijer supermarket in Greenville
- Married Fred Meijer (the grocer's son) in 1946
- Her love of gardens — especially her own rose garden at their Grand Rapids home — inspired the creation of Frederik Meijer Gardens & Sculpture Park (1995)
- The **Lena Meijer Tropical Conservatory**: 5-story, 15,000 sq ft facility
- A **three-quarter scale replica of her childhood farmhouse** stands on the grounds
- Died January 15, 2022, at age 102
- **Lena had dementia in her final years but found peace in the gardens** (parallel to Helen Walton's watercolors)

**Fred Meijer** (1919–2011):
- Co-founded Meijer Inc. with his father Hendrik in 1934
- Died November 25, 2011 of stroke complications at age 91
- Sudden — like John Walton's plane crash

**"Fred and Lena" bronze sculpture** by A. Joseph Kinkel (2004): A bench sculpture where visitors can sit between Fred and Lena. Anonymous gift.
