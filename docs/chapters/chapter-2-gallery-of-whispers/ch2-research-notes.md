## Historical Figures & Life Overlap

All three were alive simultaneously from ~1860 to 1918.

| Figure | Born | Died | Key Role |
|--------|------|------|----------|
| W.K. Kellogg | 1860 | 1951 | Built Kellogg Manor on Order-marked land; maintained sightlines |
| Bertha Honoré Palmer | 1849 | 1918 | Order patron; President, Board of Lady Managers, 1893 World's Fair |
| Mary Cassatt | 1844 | 1926 | Order advisor to Palmer; Impressionist painter |

---

## Palmer ↔ Cassatt Connection (confirmed historical)

- Sara Tyson Hallowell introduced them ~1889
- Cassatt became Palmer's personal art advisor, guiding Impressionist purchases across Europe 1891–92
- 1891: Palmer commissioned Cassatt to paint "Modern Woman" — a massive 12' × 58' mural for the Woman's Building at the 1893 World's Columbian Exposition
- Extensive correspondence between them (letters still in Art Institute archives)
- Cassatt shaped the collection that became the core of the Art Institute's Impressionist holdings
- Palmer acquired Cassatt's works when few collectors would

## Kellogg → Palmer Connection (narrative, not direct historical)

- Thomas W. Palmer (U.S. Senator from Michigan) was President of the World's Columbian Commission — different Palmer family, but Michigan-to-Chicago corridor established
- The 1893 Fair is the nexus: Bertha Palmer was the most powerful woman at the Fair, Thomas Palmer led the commission, and Kellogg was in Battle Creek/Michigan
- The 255° compass bearing from Kellogg Manor points directly toward Chicago — this is the physical sightline that connects them
- Narrative framing: Kellogg built on Order-marked land, maintained the sightlines; Palmer operated in the city those sightlines pointed toward

## Deaths

### Bertha Palmer
- **Cause:** Breast cancer
- **Date:** May 5, 1918 (age 68)
- **Location:** "The Oaks," her winter estate in Osprey, Florida
- **Details:** Fought cancer for two years, including traveling to New York for a mastectomy, then returned to Florida ranch. Body returned to Chicago; buried at Graceland Cemetery.
- **Narrative potential:** In Order lore, was it really cancer? The antagonist escalated from blinding to eliminating. Palmer was the Order's most powerful patron — removing her broke a critical link between Cassatt and the American collections.

### Mary Cassatt
- **Cause:** Natural causes / complications of diabetes
- **Date:** June 14, 1926 (age 82)
- **Location:** Château de Beaufresne, near Paris
- **Details:** Diagnosed with diabetes, cataracts, and rheumatism ~1911. Forced to stop painting entirely by 1914 as she went nearly blind. Spent her last 12 years unable to do the work that defined her. Buried at Le Mesnil-Théribus, France.
- **Narrative potential:** "The painter who saw what no one else could, losing her sight." Mirrors Kellogg's blindness. The Order believes this was not chance.

## Blindness Theme

Both **Kellogg** and **Cassatt** went blind before death. This is the Order's deepest wound — the faculty they value most ("the Sight") was taken from two of their key guardians.

- Kellogg: Lost sight in later years
- Cassatt: Cataracts forced her to stop painting by 1914; nearly blind last 12 years

**Narrative line:** "The sight is not of the eyes." — The Order believes their Sight transcends physical vision. The antagonist targeted the physical to try to destroy the metaphysical.

---

## Antagonist Escalation Arc

The antagonist is an **abstract, unnamed force** — never fully identified. The Order refers to "them" but the antagonist stays in shadow. More dread, less exposition.

### Escalation Pattern: OBSTRUCT → BLIND → KILL

1. **Obstruct** (early) — Tried to stop the Order by conventional means. Suppress, discredit, reorganize galleries, lose works, force the Order underground.
2. **Blind** (middle) — Targeted guardians' sight specifically. Cassatt (~1914) and Kellogg both lost their vision. "Taking the sight" is an attack on the Order's core power. Crueler than killing — you don't destroy the guardian, you destroy what makes them a guardian.
3. **Kill** (escalation) — Palmer dies 1918. Cancer, officially. But in Order lore, was it? She was the most powerful patron, the one who connected Cassatt to Chicago. Removing her broke a critical link.

### How the Antagonist Appears in Communications

## Chapter 2 Puzzle Design (FINAL)

### Two-Painting Journey in Gallery 273

Both paintings are in Gallery 273 at the Art Institute of Chicago. Both painted in 1893.

**Painting 1: Anders Zorn — *Mrs. Potter Palmer* (1893)**
- Q1 (Observation): "What does she hold?" → Ivory gavel
- Q2 (Appreciation): "What city appears in the inscription?" → Chicago (inscription: "Zorn / Chicago 1893")
  - Ties back to Ch1's 255° bearing pointing to Chicago

**Transition:** "The patron's eye was guided by another. Her advisor's work endures in this very room. Find it, Sparrow."

**Painting 2: Mary Cassatt — *The Child's Bath* (1893)**
- Q3 (Observation): "From what vantage does the artist compose this scene?" → From above, looking down
- Q4 (Appreciation): "What artistic tradition inspired this painting's flattened perspective?" → Japanese woodblock prints

**Backup (if The Child's Bath is off display):** Cassatt's *On a Balcony* (also Gallery 273)
- Q3-alt: "What is the woman reading?" → A newspaper
- Q4-alt: "What does the setting reveal?" → A private garden

### Key Design Decisions
- **GuidedIdentification component** — new two-phase looping component (guidance + identify). Each painting gates with identification before observation questions. Wrong answer → gentle shake → loop back to guidance. Hints available on request. Distractors re-randomize on each attempt.
- **No wayfinding component** — SMS directs to Gallery 273, GuidedIdentification narrows to each painting
- **No email during museum visit** — Christine is on the move; briefing absorbed into GuidedIdentification guidance text
- **Email briefing template (ch2-briefing.html) DEPRECATED** — preserved for reference only
- **Mostly observation** with one appreciation question per painting
- **Hint at Cassatt** without naming her — guidance hints + reward text + debrief email
- **Sister companion**: 4 messages total (tickler warning, museum protection, post-solve thanks, release)
  - Museum message emphasizes danger/vulnerability: "She is exposed now. The gallery has eyes that are not ours."
- **Painting pool** for identification MC drawn from real Gallery 273 works (Whistler, Robinson, Lungren, Breton, Renoir, etc.)

### Museum Visit Flow (Steps 4-8)
1. **GuidedIdentification (Zorn)** → Guidance clues + hints → "I think I've found it" → identify painting from randomized pool → loops until correct
2. **Zorn MultipleChoice** → 2 questions (gavel + Chicago inscription)
3. **GuidedIdentification (Cassatt)** → Guidance clues + hints → "I think I've found it" → identify painting from randomized pool → loops until correct
4. **Cassatt MultipleChoice** → 2 questions (overhead vantage + Japanese woodblock prints)
5. **RewardReveal** → "1893. Two frames. One purpose."
